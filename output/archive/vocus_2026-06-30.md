## Parse, Don't Validate – In a Language That Doesn't Want You To

Why developers may need to reevaluate their approach to validation in TypeScript.

In a recent post titled "Parse, Don't Validate – In a Language That Doesn't Want You To," developer Cekrem makes an intriguing observation about the programming language TypeScript: it seems to be telling developers not to validate, but rather to parse. This subtle distinction may seem minor at first glance, but its implications are significant, particularly for those working with this increasingly popular language.

What Happened
----------------

TypeScript, a superset of JavaScript that's widely used for building enterprise-level applications, is known for its strict type checking and strong focus on validation. However, Cekrem argues that the language's design actually prioritizes parsing over validation. In other words, when working with TypeScript, developers are encouraged to parse data rather than validate it. This shift in approach may seem counterintuitive at first, but it reflects a deeper aspect of how languages like TypeScript handle data and errors.

Why It Matters
----------------

The emphasis on parsing over validation highlights the nuances of specific programming languages and their impact on development practices. For developers accustomed to thinking about validation as the primary means of ensuring data integrity, this change in approach can be disorienting. But it also offers a valuable opportunity for growth: by understanding how different languages handle errors and data, developers can adapt their workflows and tools to suit each language's unique needs.

The Contrarian Take
-------------------

One might argue that prioritizing parsing over validation is simply a matter of semantics. After all, don't both validation and parsing share the same ultimate goal – ensuring that data is accurate and free from errors? However, proponents of this approach would contend that the distinction between parsing and validation lies in their underlying mechanics: while validation typically involves verifying the correctness of data according to predefined rules, parsing focuses on breaking down data into its constituent parts. This subtle but important difference means that developers working with languages like TypeScript must rethink their error-handling strategies.

The Core Insight
-----------------

Some languages, including TypeScript, prioritize parsing over validation because they recognize the importance of flexibility and adaptability in dealing with complex data structures. By emphasizing parsing, these languages enable developers to handle errors and exceptions more elegantly, without getting bogged down in unnecessary validation checks. This approach also opens up new possibilities for error handling, such as propagating parse errors up the call stack or using domain-specific languages (DSLs) to define custom parsing rules.

So What
----------

For developers working with TypeScript, this emphasis on parsing over validation has significant implications for their specific use cases. Rather than relying solely on validation mechanisms, they should consider the benefits of adopting a more nuanced approach that combines parsing and validation techniques. This might involve using tools like type checking and inference to identify potential errors early in the development process, while also employing more sophisticated error-handling strategies that take into account the language's unique parsing capabilities. By acknowledging the trade-offs between validation and parsing, developers can unlock new opportunities for innovation and growth in their work with TypeScript.
